Thermodynamics of the unitary Bose gas

This is a project based on path-integral quantum Monte Carlo simulations of the thermodynamics of the unitary Bose gas, which is a system of bosons with resonant two-body interactions (i.e. infinite scattering length). This system has attracted large interest as it allows to observe three-body physics at action in a many-body system, since it has provided the first experimental observation of the Efimov effect, predicted in Nuclear Physics in the 1970s. Following a previous project by Swann Piatecki, I am working on the characterization of the phase diagram and of the phase transitions which exist for a model of this system.

See:
Swann Piatecki, Werner Krauth, Efimov-driven phase transitions of the unitary Bose gas, Nature Communications 5 3503 (2014). Links: arXiv, Nature Communications (subscription required).

Classical three-body hard-core model

Hard disks and hard spheres are central models in Statistical Mechanics, and also some of the first ones to be treated by numerical methods as Monte Carlo or Molecular Dynamics. In a recent study of the unitary Bose gas in our group, the model of choice included a hard cut-off on the three-body distance (that is the root-mean-square distance of a triplet of particles); now, we have pushed this model to the regime of large temperature and large density, and studied the classical system which only includes this three-body hard-core potential (to be compared with the usual pairwise hard-core repulsion). We have studied this generalized model in two and three dimensions, and found a liquid-solid transition upon increasing pressure. The three-body interactions give rise to peculiar structures in the limit of infinite pressure: according to our conjecture, the close-packed structure is a triangular lattice of dimers in two dimensions, while it is a simple-hexagonal lattice of single particles in three dimensions. Starting from these infinite-pressure structures (which are also found through simulated annealing), we used Monte Carlo simulations in the NPT ensemble to measure the finite-pressure phase diagram. The two-dimensional solid is particularly interesting, since the dimer-dimer effective interaction can be seen at the same time as a frustrated spin-spin coupling (concerning the dimers' orientation) or as a hard-disk repulsion (concerning the dimers' positions).

See:
Tommaso Comparin, Sebastian Kapfer, Werner Krauth. Liquid-solid transitions in the three-body hard-core model, EPL 109 (2015), 20003. Links: EPL (subscription needed), arXiv.

Inhomogeneous Bose Hubbard model

Cold atoms loaded in optical lattices are regarded as clean and highly tunable set-ups, in which Condensed Matter models can be realized and studied in an easier way than in solid-state experiments. A first example of this approach has been the observation of the superfluid-to-Mott insulator phase transition in a system of ultracold 87Rb atoms, in which the predictions for the Bose-Hubbard model (bosonic equivalent of the well known Hubbard model) were experimentally realized.
During my Master thesis, we have studied this model in the set-up where the optical lattice is modified by adding an external harmonic potential, as it is often the case in the experimental realizations (although there has been recent progress on the realization of uniform box-shaped potentials, see for instance here or here). In presence of this external potential, the system becomes inhomogeneous and different domains appear, forming the so-called wedding-cake structure in the density profile. Our study is mainly based on the Gutzwiller technique, which we apply to the inhomogeneous case. In a sub-project, I have used a naive Exact Diagonalization technique and Green's Function Monte Carlo to study the one-dimensional extended Bose Hubbard model, with a focus on the Haldane phase.
In a successive study, we have addressed the problem of the Bose-Hubbard model on a two-dimensional bipartite lattice, in which the sites on two sublattices A and B (disposed as in a checkerboard) have different depths. This set-up has been experimentally realized at the Institut für Laserphysik (Hamburg), and the coherence of the system has been measured as a function of two variables: the lattice depth and the energy difference between the two sublattices (V_A < V_B, meaning that the sublattice A is favored). We used a generalized Gutzwiller ansatz for this model to provide an interpretation of the experimental results. We find that when the energy difference V_B-V_A is large, the population of the sublattice B vanishes, and bosons form a Mott-insulating phase for the effective model which only includes sites A.
Moreover, the behavior of the experimental curve for the monopartite case (V_A=V_B) is interpreted using the LDA estimates of the critical couplings (obtained through a Monte Carlo technique).

See:
